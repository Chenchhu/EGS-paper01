A B S T R A C T

Geothermal resources have important value for its development and utilization. In this work, a thermal- -hydraulic-mechanical (THM) coupled numerical model is developed to simulate the heat extraction process in an enhanced geothermal system (EGS). In particular, the matrix permeability enhancement is related to thermal unloading, and the fracture conductivity is determined by the normal effective stress state. Then, a theoretical solution and a field measurement are selected to validate the accuracy/practicality of the THM coupled model. Afterwards, two simulation cases are designed to discuss the effect of natural fractures on the heat extraction performance. Firstly, the EGS contains only one natural fracture. The heat exchange and the temperature distribution in the natural fracture and rock matrix are greatly affected by the location of the natural fracture. Secondly, the EGS contains multiple natural fractures. The results indicate that more natural fractures improve the performance of heat exchange in middle natural fracture and generate a larger cooling area in the rock matrix. With an increase in the fracture number in the EGS, the influence of the included angle between the hydraulic fracture and natural fracture on the production temperature becomes more significant, and the heat extraction efficiency is greatly improved.

1. Introduction

Geothermal energy is worldly distributed with great reserves and is becoming increasingly attractive for meeting the demand for energy due to its eco-friendly and sustainable properties [1-3]. The development of geothermal energy is of significance for lowering the dependence on expensive and environmentally unfriendly fossil fuels, and, thus, is helpful for achieving a low-carbon environment [4-8]. Basically, geothermal energy originates from underground hot dry rock (HDR), which is usually characterized as tight with low permeability, for example, granite [9-11]. As a result, this type of tight geothermal reservoir needs to be stimulated to form an enhanced geothermal system (EGS). Then, the purpose of commercial utilization can be realized. To ensure sufficient heat exchange between geothermal reservoirs and injected fluids, hydraulic fracturing is a common in situ technique for creating flow and heat transfer channels for the injected fluid [12-15].
Although some in situ projects have been conducted and some beneficial conclusions have been obtained, it is still challenging to understand the full heat exchange process in EGSs [15-19]. As an effective method, numerical simulation can be utilized to help researchers and engineers analyze the heat extraction process [20-24]. Generally, heat extraction in a reservoir is a complicated process that involves several interactive physical processes, such as rock deformation, fluid flow and heat transfer. Based on the thermal-hydraulic (TH) or thermal- -hydraulic-mechanical (THM) coupled model, some studies have been conducted to investigate the heat extraction process in EGSs, especially the influence of hydraulic fractures. For example, Aliyu and Archer [25] and Aliyu and Archer [26] established a multiple hydraulic fracture model and a single hydraulic fracture model and then analyzed the different factors (e.g., fracture number and fracture spacing) affecting reservoir performance during exploitation. Han et al. [27] found that insufficient/excessive fracturing stages are contradictory to economic heat utilization, and an optimized number of fracturing segments can be designed for a specified wellbore length. Zhang et al. [28] assumed that hydraulic fractures are characterized as fractal-like tree features and discussed how the geometric and heterogeneous properties affect heat extraction. These numerical results showed that hydraulic fractures can greatly improve geothermal energy production in reservoirs with low permeability.

Natural fractures are a common discontinuous plane existing in reservoirs. These fractures can also provide flow and heat transfer channels for the fluid [21,29-32]. During the hydraulic fracturing process, when a hydraulic fracture intersects with a natural fracture, three types of behaviour can occur, namely, the hydraulic fracture crosses the natural fracture, the hydraulic fracture is arrested by the natural fracture or the hydraulic fracture diverts into the natural fracture. The intersection between a hydraulic fracture and a natural fracture is advantageous for forming a fracture network [33]. On the basis of the hydraulic fracturing technique, researchers have paid more attention to the fracture network. Guo et al. [34] and Guo et al. [35] developed a 3D THM coupled model to discuss the performance of EGSs under the background of fractured geothermal reservoirs. The natural fractures were randomly distributed in the reservoir at different scales. The results showed that the formation of connected fractures can increase the heat extraction efficiency. Zhou et al. [36] designed natural fractures with variable apertures in a discrete fracture network (DFN) and concluded that the variation characteristics for DFN apertures strongly affect the performance of geothermal reservoirs. In the DFN model developed by Shi et al. [37], secondary hydraulic fractures were also imbedded into the networks. They compared the contributions of primary and secondary fractures to heat extraction and suggested that the fracturing operation should focus on creating longer secondary fractures to connect natural fractures far away from lateral wells.
According to the studies mentioned above, an appropriate DFN can certainly enhance the efficiency and economics of heat extraction. However, the lithology of dry hot rock is mainly granite or dense metamorphic rock. Unlike the rock mass of oil or gas reservoirs [38,39], dry hot rock has the physical properties of high hardness and ultra-tightness [40]. On the other hand, the depth of current typical EGS hydraulic fracturing (e.g., Eenton Hill EGS, Soultz EGS, Cooper Basin EGS,Pohang EGS) can reach more than 3000m . High in situ stress is detrimental to microcrack generation. Therefore, it is difficult to form complex fracture networks by fracturing in HDR reservoirs. In addition, the previous work noted that a series of hybrid tensile-shear failure events enables a complex fracture network that consists of both reactivated natural fractures and newly created tensile fractures [4]. That is to say, when HDR is stimulated by hydraulic fracturing, the fracture system is more likely to consist of hydraulic fractures and natural fractures rather than a complex DFN. Thus, the influence of the natural fracture characteristics on the heat extraction performance is very significant. Unfortunately, to our knowledge, most studies have mainly focused on the random DFN model in the heat extraction process. Because there are a great number of natural fractures randomly distributed in this model, how natural fractures affect fluid flow and heat transfer in the hydraulic fracture and matrix remains unclear. Existing conclusions can sparingly be used as a responsible guidance for fracture systems that consist of hydraulic fractures and a few natural fractures.

In this study, a THM coupled three-dimensional model was developed in COMSOL software. The permeability evolution of the rock matrix and fracture is of great significance for heat extraction in an EGS. Unlike the previous numerical model, the matrix permeability enhancement is related to thermal unloading, and the fracture conductivity is determined by the normal effective stress state. Then, the theoretical solution combined with a field measurement, located at Fenton Hill Phase I site, was introduced to support the reliability and acceptance regarding the THM coupled model. According to the reliable model, different natural fracture distributions were designed as for the EGS project, by which how the natural fractures exert influence on the extraction of heat in EGS is analyzed in a comprehensive manner. Hopefully, this work can provide a robust reference for the geothermal engineering design in the reservoirs containing natural fractures with a fresh perspective.

2. THM coupled model

2.1. Model assumptions

To simulate the heat extraction in HDR, a three-dimensional THM numerical model is developed in the study. Several assumptions are made for the process of rock deformation, fluid flow and heat transfer, which are also adopted by some previous works [18,35,41] that support the reliability of this modeling work.
(1) With the help of the hydraulic fracturing technique, the EGS reservoir consists of a porous matrix, natural fractures and artificial fractures. The properties of the porous matrix are homogeneous, elastic and isotropic. The fractures are embedded in the matrix with lower-dimensional entities.
(2) During the heat extraction process, water is utilized as the working fluid and exists in liquid form in the pores and fractures.

(3) After hydraulic fracturing, water saturates the EGS, and the resulting fluid flow in the matrix and fracture is regarded as laminar flow and obeys Darcy's law.
(4) Fourier's law is used to describe the heat transfer process in the matrix and fracture. Local thermal equilibrium is assumed between the rock mass and working fluid.
(5) Pressure is used to induce variation in the fracture conductivity. However, the effect of the stress field on the matrix permeability is ignored. If a sudden alteration occurs to the rock's temperature, the stress field is induced and thus the permeability as for the matrix will be enhanced.
(6) For the conditions of the THM process, the chemical reaction, as well as the fracture propagation, is not involved in the numerical simulation.

2.3. Evolution of the permeability

During the heat extraction operation, the permeability of the porous medium and the fracture is of great significance. When cryogenic working fluid is introduced into the HDR, a sudden alteration goes to the rock surface temperature and thus can induce internal damage. As a result, the permeability variation needs to be involved for the heat extraction behavior. For example, the laboratory experiments conducted by Cheng [45] exhibits the measured permeability alteration when granite experienced the thermal unloading operation. In this work, two parameters are set for describing how the thermal unloading affect the permeability, that is, 1) the ratio of water cooling-induced permeability ( kc ) to the original permeability ( ki ),marked as R ,and 2) the difference in temperature between the injected cryogenic water and the heated rock,labeled as ΔT . 3. Validation of the numerical simulation model

To develop a responsible model, a field measurement was selected to support the reliability of the THM coupled model herein, which is located at the Fenton Hill Phase I site and is treated as the first EGS project all over the world. Therein, in that project, the injection pressure and production temperature have been recorded [48]. According to the field conditions, a conceptual EGS model is established [48], where a vertical hydraulic fracture with a diameter of 120m was created in the center of this EGS reservoir and an injection well was set below the production well (Fig. 3). The buried depth of the two wells was 2750m and 2670m ,respectively. Besides,the production well and injection well is 25m far away from the fracture roof and fracture bottom, respectively.
With regard to the reservoir, the geological conditions were: (1) vertical stress, 74MPa ; minimum/maximum horizontal stress, 37MPa / 53 Mpa [25]; (2) rock temperature at the injection well, 458.15K ; (3) geothermal gradient, 373.15K/km ; and (4) pore pressure, ∼27MPa .

5. Results and discussion

5.1. Case 1: EGS with one natural fracture

5.1.1. Heat extraction performance in single natural fracture

For case 1, only one natural fracture is considered in the EGS. Herein, we mainly discuss the effect of two parameters,namely, dis and β ,on the heat extraction. According to different values of dis and β ,nine working conditions are calculated. In the numerical model, injected fluid moves into the EGS only via the perforation. Accordingly, the temperature of
hydraulic fracture surface is quickly decreased, and this temperature alteration is similar as for different simulation conditions, caused by the continuous fluid injection. However, the temperature distribution in the natural fracture is greatly affected by dis. At the intersection of the two fractures, part of the cryogenic fluid flows into the natural fracture. Hence, the inflow amount of cryogenic fluid is an important factor affecting heat exchange in natural fractures. Taking β=0∘ as an example, it can be observed that when the natural fracture is close to the injection wellbore ( dis=75m ),heat exchange is relatively sufficient (Fig. 8a). After 40 years of cryogenic fluid injection, the fracture surface temperature ranges from 304K to 470K ,and the average temperature is 348K . The low-temperature area is elliptical. When dis is increased to 150m (located at the middle of the two wellbores),the pressure gradient along the y -axis becomes much smaller (Fig. 8b). According to Darcy’s law, the inflow amount of cryogenic fluid is also small. After 40 years of cryogenic fluid injection, the fracture surface temperature ranges from 307K to 540K ,and the average temperature is 421K . Through a comparison of the temperature distribution and average temperature, it can be seen that the heat extraction efficiency decreases quickly as dis increases from 75m to 150m . As the value of dis is 225m ,the temperature distribution is different from the former two conditions (Fig. 8a). The main reason for this is due to the distribution of the pressure gradient. When the natural fracture is close to the production wellbore, two boundaries cannot be ignored: (1) the production pressure remains at 40MPa ,which is the lowest pressure in the EGS,and (2) the hydraulic fracture tip is impermeable except at the location of the production wellbore. Then, the pressure gradient result is opposite to that of the other two conditions (Fig. 8b). Along the direction of the fracture height, the width of the low-temperature area in the middle of the fracture surface is small. However, in the upper and lower areas of the surface, the width is large. After 40 years of cryogenic fluid injection, the fracture surface temperature ranges from 310K to 475K ,and the average temperature is 372K . The low-temperature area is dumbbell-shaped. In addition,for β=30∘ or 60∘ ,the temperature distribution also shows a more sufficient heat exchange when the natural fracture is closer to the injection wellbore,which is similar to that found when β= 0∘ . In summary,the parameter d is significantly influence the heat exchange in natural fractures. When the existence of natural fracture in the middle of the two wellbores tends to enable a relatively low efficiency of heat exchange, compared the situation without nature fractures [41].

5.1.2. Heat extraction performance in a porous medium

During the heat extraction process, part of the cryogenic fluid flows from the fracture to the rock matrix due to the pressure difference. Then, heat exchange is realized in the porous medium around the fracture. To discuss the influence of the natural fracture, two horizontal section planes at depths of 4700m(H1 section) and 4620m(H2 section ) were selected to analyze the temperature distribution. The temperature distribution in the two sections after 40 years of fluid injection is shown in Fig. 9. The existence of natural fractures inevitably affects the shape of the low-temperature area. Simultaneously, the difference in the temperature distribution in the H1 section and H2 section is also affected by the shape of the low-temperature area in the natural fractures. For example,when dis is 75m ,the low-temperature area around the natural fracture in the H1 section is larger than that in the H2 section. When dis is 225m ,the opposite result is obtained. When d is is 150m ,the force of the natural fracture influencing the cooling area is limited and can even be ignored (β=60∘) .
To analyze the temperature distribution in the rock matrix in the vertical direction, a section plane (Section V) parallel to the hydraulic fracture was selected. The distance between the two planes is 20m . When β remains constant,the effect of dis is similar. Herein, β=0∘ is taken as an example. As shown in Fig. 10, the low-temperature area is located mainly around the natural fracture. Basically, the temperature distribution is significantly influenced by the natural fracture. The temperature alteration along two straight lines within the range from −150m to 150m along the x -axis is shown in Fig. 11. The depths of Line A and Line B are 4700m and 4650m ,respectively. Along the direction from the injection well to the production well, the temperature curves are V-shaped, namely, they first show a decrease followed by an increase. The lowest temperature (Tlow ) occurs at the location of the natural fracture. Apart from dis=150m ,the variation trends for decreasing and increasing temperatures are different. For example, when dis is 75 m ,the temperature rapidly decreases linearly. However,in the stage of increasing temperature, the rapid stage shows a fast-slow-fast characteristic. When dis is 225m ,the decreasing temperature follows a fast-slow-fast trend, and then the temperature increases linearly. At both ends of the two lines (x=−150mand150m) ,the values of temperature obtained from models with different d is values are relatively close. When dis is 75m or 150m,Tlow  in Line A is slightly lower than that in Line B. Due to the influence of the natural fracture,when dis is 225m,Tlow  in Line A is higher than that in Line B . In general,compared with the other two conditions, the average temperature of the area around the natural fracture is higher when dis is 150m . With regard to the area far from the natural fracture, the values of temperature are relatively close for the three conditions.

As for the alteration from nine simulation conditions share a similar tendency in Tout  along with time,that is, Tout  rapidly decreases during the early stage of fluid injection. Under the nine combinations of d is and β ,the rates of decrease are different. The variation in Tout  within 5 years is shown in Figs. 12 and 13. When d is remains constant,the curves are nearly identical for β=0∘ and 30∘ . However,as β is increased to 60∘ ,the angle between the hydraulic fracture and natural fracture decreases. It is more conducive for cryogenic fluid to flow towards the production wellbore. Hence, Tout  decreases more quickly than that for the other two conditions (Fig. 12). The values of the maximum temperature difference are 37K(dis=75m),33K(dis=150m) ,and 20K(dis=225m) . As the natural fracture approaches the production wellbore,the effect of β on Tout  can be ignored.
When β remains constant,the temperature decline rates are similar for the three values of dis (Fig. 13). For the case of dis=150m ,because less cryogenic fluid leaks off the natural fracture, Tout  is slightly lower than that for the other two conditions. The values for the maximum temperature difference are 16K(β=0∘),17K(β=30∘) ,and 24K(β= 60∘ ). With the continuous injection of cryogenic fluid, Tout  decreases much more gently. After 40 years of injection, Tout  for the nine simulation cases is approximately equal to 318K .

During the simulated 40 years of the fluid injection, the increase rates for η are first rapid and then slow. For the stage of a rapid increase, the variation in η is nearly the same under the different combinations of dis and β . For the stage of a slow increase, η is slightly affected by dis and β . Finally, η ranges from 36% to 42% within the nine numerical models. η relates to the heat exchange in the fracture and rock matrix. As show in Fig. 9,the smaller the β ,the larger the cooling area. When dis remains constant, η increases with for a lower β (Fig. 14). The effect of β can be ignored for dis=150m . Under the simulation conditions of three values of dis, a smaller amount of cryogenic fluid enters into the natural fracture for dis=150m . When β remains constant,the value of η is the lowest for the case of dis=150m (Fig. 15). For the other two values of dis ,the curves are similar,especially for β=60∘ .

5.2. Case 2: EGS with multiple natural fractures

5.2.1. Heat extraction performance in multiple natural fractures

In case 2, multiple natural fractures are considered in the EGS. In total,three types of fracture numbers (N=1,2and 3) are employed. Combined with three different values of β ,nine working conditions are simulated. Compared to β ,the N influences the heat extraction performance in natural fractures,in a more obvious manner. Taking β=0∘ as an example, the temperature distribution in natural fractures is shown in Fig. 16. In view of the symmetry of the temperature distribution, only half of the fracture surfaces are given in Fig. 16. As analyzed in case 1, dis is a key factor in determining the low-temperature area in the natural fracture. In the EGS with multiple natural fractures, the low-temperature area is also affected by N . As shown in Fig. 16,for the natural fracture located in the middle (dis =150m ),the cooling area shape changes with increasing N . Along the height direction,it appears mainly in the narrowing of the middle area and the widening of the upper and lower areas. Hence,the area shape changes from an ellipse (N=1) to a rectangle (N=3) and then to a dumbbell (N=5) . In addition,after 40 years of cryogenic fluid injection, the average temperature of the middle fracture is approximately 425K for N=1 and N=3 . For the case of N= 5,the average temperature decreases to 410K . This means that more natural fractures accelerate the heat exchange in the middle natural fracture. For β=30∘ or 60∘ ,the temperature distribution in natural fractures displays similar features for varying N .

5.2.2. Heat extraction performance in a porous medium

To analyze the heat extraction process in the EGS with multiple fractures,the temperature distribution in the H1 and H2 sections after 40 years of cryogenic fluid injection is shown in Fig. 17. For N=1 ,the existence of natural fracture can be ignored,especially in the H2 section. Under the condition of multiple natural fractures, the low-temperature areas are mainly concentrated around the natural fractures except for the middle fracture. Hence, more natural fractures generate a larger cooling area. In addition, the difference in the low-temperature areas in the H1 and H2 sections is also affected by the cooling area shape of the natural fractures. For example,when N=5 ,the cooling area near the injection wellbore is greater than that near the production wellbore in Section H1 . However,the relative size of the low-temperature area is opposite in the H2 section.
After 40 years of cryogenic fluid injection, the temperature distribution within the porous media in Section V is shown in Fig. 18(β=0∘) . Similarly, the low-temperature area is mainly located around the natural fractures along the vertical direction. The area becomes larger as N increases for the following two reasons: (1) More natural fractures are advantageous for extending the contact area between fractures and the rock matrix. Then, a greater amount of cryogenic fluid leak-off occurs and enhances the heat exchange range. (2) Once the areas around the natural fractures are cooled, the matrix permeability increases. This results in a greater amount of cryogenic fluid flowing towards the farther hot area.
After 40 years of cryogenic fluid injection, the temperature variation along Line A and Line B is shown in Fig. 19. For N=3 or 5,the curves are V-shaped along Line A. Similarly, the temperature rapidly decreases linearly and then increases with a fast-slow-fast characteristic. The values for Tlow  are nearly identical. Compared to the case with N=1 ,the difference in Tlow  can reach up to 45K . For N=3 or 5 along Line B,the curves are W-shaped. Except for the centre region (ranging from −75m to 75m ),the two curves are almost coincident. As analyzed above,the more natural fractures, the more advantageous for heat extraction behavior in the EGS. Therefore, as for the situation of multi-fractures, the temperature calculated under the condition with N=5 is lower than that with N=3 . In general,the natural fracture number plays a significant role in cooling the rock matrix. It is beneficial to enhance the heat exchange process.

5.2.3. Heat extraction indices

The effect of N and β on the variation in Tout  within 5 years is shown in Figs. 20 and 21. Whether the EGS contains a single natural fracture or multiple natural fractures,the curves obtained for the cases of low β(β =0∘ and 30∘ ) are almost identical. For high β(β=60∘),Tout  rapidly decreases in the early stage. When N remains constant,the maximum temperature differences between low β and high β are 33K(N=1),69K (N=3) and 80K(N=5) ,respectively. It can be observed that with an increase in N in the EGS,the influence of β becomes more significant.
When β is 0∘ or 30∘ ,compared to the case with N=1 ,multiple natural fractures in the EGS can slow the rate of decrease for Tout  (Fig. 21a and b). The curves are similar for the case of multiple natural fractures. With increasing N,Tout  is slightly higher. When β is 60∘ ,within the first 1.2 years of fluid injection,the rate of decrease of Tout  increases with higher N . However,in the later injection process,the rate of decrease of Tout  shows an opposite variation,namely,it decreases with higher N . Then, Tout  obtained for the case of multiple natural fractures is similar and higher than that for the case with N=1 (Fig. 21c). In addition,when β remains constant,the values for the maximum temperature difference are 37K(β=0∘),38K(β=30∘) ,and 27K(β=60∘) . In general,with increasing β ,the influence of the fracture number in the EGS is relatively similar.
The effect of N and β on the variation in η within 40 years is shown in Figs. 22 and 23. When N=1,η is almost the same for different β values (Fig. 22a). With more natural fractures in the EGS,the values of η increase,and the influence of β becomes obvious (Fig. 22b and c). For example,when N=5 ,the maximum η is 51.6%(β=0∘) and the minimum η is 42.5%(β=60∘) . The difference in η can reach up to 9% , which is greater than that obtained for the case with a single natural fracture. Compared with β ,the effect of N on η curves is more significant. The heat extraction efficiency increases with greater N . For low β ,after 40 years of cryogenic fluid injection,the values for η range from 36.7% to 51.5%(β=0∘) and 36.5%−49.4%(β=30∘) . The differences in η are 15% and 13% ,respectively. For high β ,the difference in η decreases to 7%(β=60∘) . Thus,it can be concluded that with increasing N ,both the heat extraction efficiency and the difference in η (between high β and low β ) improve greatly.

6. Summary and conclusions

In this study, a validated and responsible THM coupled model is developed to simulate heat extraction behavior in an EGS, by which the natural fracture influencing the heat extraction performance is investigated insightfully. The main conclusions yield.
(1) The temperature distribution in the natural fracture is greatly affected by dis . With an increase in dis ,the low-temperature area changes from elliptical-shaped to dumbbell-shaped. When the natural fracture is set near the middle of two wellbores, the resulting heat exchange efficiency is relatively low. The existence of natural fractures and their low-temperature shape inevitably affect the temperature distribution in the rock matrix. Along the direction from the injection wellbore to the production wellbore, the temperature curves are V-shaped. However, the increasing or increasing trend depends on dis.
(2) When dis remains constant,the Tout  curves are nearly identical for β=0∘ and 30∘ . As β is increased to 60∘,Tout  decreases more rapidly in the early injection stage. When β is kept constant,the temperature decline rates are similar for the three values of dis. With regard to η ,in the stage of rapid increase,the variation of η is nearly the same under the different combinations of dis and β ; in the stage of a slow increase, η is slightly affected by dis and β .
(3) The low-temperature area is greatly affected by N . For the natural fracture located in the middle, the cooling area shape changes from elliptical (N=1) to rectangular (N=3) and then to a dumbbell (N=5) . More natural fractures promotes the heat exchange and thus ensure a larger cooling area. For N=3 or 5 along Line A ,the curves are V-shaped. However,the curves are W-shaped along Line B. Except for the centre region, the two curves are almost coincident. In the centre region, the temperature calculated under the condition with N=5 is lower than that with N=3 .
(4) With increasing N in the EGS,the influence of β on Tout  becomes more significant. When β is 0∘ or 30∘ ,multiple natural fractures in the EGS can slow the rate of decrease of Tout  . When β is 60∘ ,the rate of decrease of Tout  first increases and then decreases with increasing N . With more natural fractures in the EGS,both the heat extraction efficiency and the difference in η (between high β and low β ) shows a great improvement.
